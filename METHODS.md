# Residue-Prioritization Algorithms — Detailed Methods

This document describes the four browser-side residue-prioritization routines implemented in the 3D Feature Viewer for UniProt (v2.0.0). All four are exploratory heuristics intended to guide visual structural interpretation and hypothesis generation; none is a clinical classifier or a validated pathogenicity predictor. Results should be interpreted together with the underlying variant annotations, structural context, and available experimental evidence.

---

## 1. Pathogenic-variant enrichment hotspots

### Rationale

Disease-associated and functional missense variants can show non-random spatial clustering in protein structures. Residues that are densely surrounded by pathogenic or deleterious variants in 3D are candidate functional or structurally important sites. This observation underlies methods such as HotMAPS [1], HotSpot3D [2], 3D-Clustering [3], and the germline-pathogenic spatial enrichment analysis of Sivley et al. [4].

### Variant classification

Variants are collapsed to residue-level labels. A residue is labelled **pathogenic/deleterious** if it carries at least one clinical annotation matching `pathogenic`, or if at least one computational prediction is labelled `deleterious`, `damaging`, or `pathogenic` (case-insensitive). A residue is labelled **benign-only** if it carries a benign clinical annotation and no pathogenic/deleterious annotation. Residues with only ambiguous or uncertain annotations are excluded from enrichment calculations.

### Null model selection

Two complementary nulls are available, selected automatically:

- **Case–control label-permutation null** (preferred): used when the protein has ≥ 3 pathogenic/deleterious and ≥ 2 benign-only labelled residues. This null asks whether pathogenic labels are spatially clustered relative to benign labels, controlling for which residues were studied or sequenced.
- **Spatial-placement null** (fallback for benign-poor proteins): used when fewer than 2 benign-only residues are available. This null asks whether pathogenic residues cluster more than expected if the same number were placed uniformly at random over all modelled residues of the chain, requiring no benign controls. This is the approach of HotMAPS [1] and Kamburov et al. [3]. Because it does not control for ascertainment bias, results from this null are labelled *spatial clustering* in the legend and should be interpreted with slightly more caution.

### Case–control label-permutation test

For each chain of the structure, the set of classified residues (pathogenic/deleterious and benign-only) whose Cα atoms are modelled is assembled. The observed statistic for each pathogenic residue *i* is its **local pathogenic density** *d*(*i*): the number of classified residues (including itself) with Cα–Cα distance ≤ 8 Å that carry a pathogenic/deleterious label.

An empirical null distribution for *d*(*i*) is generated by randomly permuting the pathogenic/benign labels over the fixed set of classified residue positions 1,000 times. At each permutation the local pathogenic density is recomputed for all residues. The empirical p-value for residue *i* is:

$$
p_i = \frac{\#\{d_i^{(\mathrm{perm})} \geq d_i^{(\mathrm{obs})}\} + 1}{1{,}000 + 1}.
$$

The +1 estimator (Phipson & Smyth [5]) ensures p is never exactly zero. Because overlapping neighbourhoods make the per-residue tests non-independent—a well-known issue with neighbourhood-based tests that invalidates parametric approaches [3]—the permutation scheme correctly respects the spatial autocorrelation structure without requiring independence.

Multiple testing is controlled with the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure [6] applied to pathogenic-centre p-values within each chain, yielding FDR-adjusted q-values.

### Spatial-placement null (fallback)

When benign-only controls are unavailable, the null is that the *m* pathogenic residues are placed uniformly at random over the *N* modelled residues of the chain. At each of 1,000 permutations, *m* residue indices are chosen uniformly without replacement using Fisher–Yates shuffling, and local pathogenic density is recomputed for each observed pathogenic residue. The empirical p and BH correction are computed as above. This fallback is run only for chains with 10–6,000 modelled residues.

### Partner-protein disease context (hetero-complexes)

For experimental structures of multi-protein complexes (e.g. GABA-A receptor pentamers), the pathogenic and benign residues of neighbouring partner proteins are fetched from the UniProt variation API, mapped to their author residue numbers in the PDB structure via SIFTS, and included as additional spatial points in the permutation pool. To bound network use on large hetero-complexes, variants are fetched for at most eight distinct partner accessions. This allows a residue of the protein of interest that is physically close to a cluster of pathogenic residues on a partner subunit to be scored accordingly. Partner residues are never visualised and do not appear in any output; only residues of the queried accession are reported.

### Tier assignment

| Tier | Case–control null | Spatial-placement null |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong** | q ≤ 0.10 AND *d* ≥ 3 AND enrichment ratio ≥ 2.0 | q ≤ 0.10 AND *d* ≥ 3 |
| **Moderate** | q ≤ 0.25 AND *d* ≥ 2 AND enrichment ratio ≥ 1.5 | q ≤ 0.25 AND *d* ≥ 2 |
| **Weak** | q ≤ 0.25 AND *d* ≥ 2 AND local pathogenic fraction ≥ 0.40 | — |

The enrichment ratio is the local pathogenic fraction divided by the global pathogenic fraction across the full classified pool (including partner points when present). For multi-chain structures, each chain is tiered independently; the merged view shows the highest tier seen across chains at each UniProt position.

### Validity gate

The permutation test is not run when fewer than 3 pathogenic residues are present (regardless of benign count). Such proteins return an empty hotspot map.

### Reproducibility

The permutation PRNG is a seeded mulberry32 generator (seed `0x9e3779b9`), so results are identical across recomputes and browser sessions.

## 2. Long-range contact hubs

### Rationale

Protein structures have been represented as residue-interaction networks to study folding, functional residues, stability, and short-path communication [7–9]. The present routine uses betweenness centrality as a transparent bridge score. Its exact graph construction and z-score tiers are specific to this extension. This is distinct from raw contact degree, which largely reflects burial in the hydrophobic core and is a confound for identifying structurally meaningful bridging positions.

### Residue contact graph

A contact graph is constructed with one node per modelled Cα atom. An undirected edge is placed between nodes *i* and *j* if their Cα–Cα distance is ≤ 8 Å. The graph is unweighted. For multi-chain structures, each chain of the protein of interest is analysed independently (inter-chain contact graphs are not constructed).

### Betweenness centrality

Betweenness centrality is computed with Brandes' exact algorithm [9]:

$$
BC(v) =
\frac{1}{(N-1)(N-2)}
\sum_{\substack{s \neq v \neq t \\ s \neq t}}
\frac{\sigma_{st}(v)}{\sigma_{st}}.
$$

where $\sigma_{st}$ is the number of shortest paths between nodes $s$ and $t$, and $\sigma_{st}(v)$ is the number of those paths that pass through $v$. The code normalises the ordered-pair sum for an undirected graph by dividing by $(N-1)(N-2)$, where $N$ is the number of nodes.

### Significance threshold

Rather than always flagging a fixed percentage of residues (which guarantees output even for featureless structures), hubs are assigned by an absolute threshold on the z-score of the betweenness distribution within the chain:

$$
z(v) = \frac{BC(v) - \mu_{BC}}{\sigma_{BC}}.
$$

where μ and σ are the mean and standard deviation of betweenness across all chain nodes. Nodes with z ≥ 3.0 are labelled **strong** contact hubs; nodes with z ≥ 2.0 are labelled **moderate**. A structurally uniform chain (e.g. a coiled-coil with homogeneous packing) will produce no hub calls.

### Interpretation

This score is a structural-network heuristic, not a formal statistical test and not an inferred allosteric network. Its purpose is to identify residues with exceptional connectivity in the contact graph for rapid visual inspection and follow-up. Chains with fewer than 8 residues are excluded.

## 3. Mutation/phenotype burden residues

### Rationale

Recurrence of variant observations at the same amino-acid position, especially across distinct disease or phenotypic annotations, can indicate a residue that warrants closer structural and functional investigation [11,12]. This analysis mirrors the multi-metric hotspot prioritization used in Akbari Ahangar et al. [13], which ranked positions in voltage-gated sodium channels by number of phenotypes, number of mutations, and cross-protein recurrence at equivalent positions.

### Per-position metrics

For each variant-containing residue position the extension computes:

1. **Mutation count** (*C*): number of distinct variant records annotated at this position.
2. **Phenotype diversity** (*P*): number of distinct disease or phenotype labels associated with variants at this position, counted using disease identifiers where available and disease labels otherwise.

### Percentile-rank composite

Each metric is converted to a **percentile rank** across all variant-containing positions in the protein: the fraction of positions with a value ≤ the position's value. Percentile ranking is robust to the small, skewed count distributions typical of variant data and does not depend on choosing absolute cutoffs.

The composite score is the unweighted mean of the two percentile ranks:

$$
S_i = \frac{1}{2}\operatorname{rank}(C_i) + \frac{1}{2}\operatorname{rank}(P_i).
$$

### Absolute floors and flagging

A position is labelled **burden-positive** if:

- *C* ≥ 2 (at least two distinct variant records), AND
- *P* ≥ 2 (at least two distinct disease/phenotype labels), AND
- composite score S ≥ 0.90 (top decile)

The absolute floors prevent singletons or single-phenotype positions from being flagged purely on the basis of a high relative rank in a sparse protein. The output is a binary flag; it is not a statistical test and does not yield a p-value.

### Limitations

Both *C* and *P* reflect reporting intensity: frequently studied positions (e.g. canonical active-site residues, CpG-prone codons) accumulate more records independent of true pathogenic density. The phenotype-diversity component partially mitigates this, as ascertainment at multiple independent clinical contexts is less likely to reflect a pure reporting artefact. A third metric — recurrence at equivalent positions across paralogous proteins — would further reduce ascertainment bias [13] but requires a family/MSA alignment not available from a single UniProt entry and is not currently implemented.

## 4. AlphaMissense residue scores

AlphaMissense residue scores are not computed by this extension. Where available, the full per-substitution CSV from the AlphaFold DB is fetched and used to compute per-position summaries across all available amino-acid substitutions: the mean score, maximum score, and number of scored substitutions. The average score is displayed as a color overlay; average and maximum scores are included in the exported CSV. Score interpretation follows the published thresholds: likely pathogenic ≥ 0.564, ambiguous 0.34–0.564, likely benign < 0.34 [14].

### Reference

14. Cheng J et al. Accurate proteome-wide missense variant effect prediction with AlphaMissense. *Science* 2023; 381(6664):eadg7492. doi:10.1126/science.adg7492.

## General notes on all prioritization outputs

- All algorithms operate on the structure currently loaded in the viewer. Switching to a different structure recomputes all distance-based analyses for that structure.
- For multi-chain structures each chain is analysed independently; the merged view shows the highest tier across chains.
- Residue numbers in all outputs are reported in UniProt sequence coordinates after structure-to-UniProt mapping via SIFTS or the AlphaFold 1:1 scheme.
- None of these outputs constitute clinical evidence. They are intended to support visual triage and the generation of hypotheses for experimental follow-up.
